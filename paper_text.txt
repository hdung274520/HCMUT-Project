Abstract.Wearable electroencephalography (EEG) devices enable continuous and non-
invasive monitoring of brain activity in real-world environments. However, the limited number
of channels, the use of dry electrodes, and susceptibility to motion and environmental noise
pose major challenges for reliable mental state recognition. This paper provides a compre-
hensive analysis of engineering features and model comparision to classify mental states,
specifically ‘Focus’, ‘Unfocus’, and ‘Relaxation’ conditions, from a wearable EEG dataset.
In particular, we systematically examine statistical and spectral features to identify those
most informative to discriminate between cognitive states. Then, multiple machine learning
models, including traditional machine learning and deep neural networks, are evaluated un-
der session-dependent, session-independent, and subject-independent protocols. Experimental
results reveal that high detection performance can be achieved even with a few EEG channels.
Keywords:EEG, Focus Detection, Brain–Computer Interface
1 Introduction
One of the most important mental states is Focus, which refers to the sustained allocation of at-
tention toward a specific task or stimulus while actively filtering out irrelevant information and
distractions [1]. It represents a dynamic mental state that fluctuates with task difficulty, motiva-
tion, and fatigue, often declining as cognitive resources are depleted or mind-wandering occurs.
In contrast, Relaxation denotes a state of reduced physiological and mental arousal, characterized
by slower neural activity and dominance of the parasympathetic nervous system [2–5]. These two
mental states, Focus and Relaxation, play crucial roles in cognitive performance, emotional regula-
tion, and overall mental health. As interest in decoding and monitoring mental states grows across
domains such as neurotechnology, education, healthcare, and human-machine interaction, wearable
electroencephalography (EEG) systems have emerged as a promising tool for real-time assessment

of brain activity [6]. These devices offer a non-invasive means to measure electrical brain activ-
ity, providing a window into cognitive and emotional processes in ecologically valid settings. This
capability is crucial for advancing research in various domains, from mental health monitoring to
enhancing human-computer interaction, by enabling continuous data collection [7]. Moreover, the
integration of machine learning (ML) with EEG data analysis has revolutionized the ability to inter-
pret these complex neural signals, allowing for automated recognition and classification of diverse
mental states. This synergy facilitates the transition from raw neural data to actionable insights,
enabling applications such as real-time mental workload assessment, emotion detection, and even
augmented reality interfaces driven by brain-computer interaction (BCI) [8,9]. This convergence
of wearable EEG technology and advanced ML algorithms thus paves the way for sophisticated
passive BCIs that monitor users’ mental states in real-time applications [10,11].
However, wearable EEG devices are typically implemented with dry electrodes and a limited
number of recording channels, and are designed to be worn during routine daily activities. The
constrained electrode count inherently limits spatial resolution. Furthermore, dry electrodes often
exhibit higher and more variable contact impedance compared to wet electrodes, which can degrade
signal quality and increase susceptibility to noise. Motion artifacts, resulting from both electrode-
skin interface disturbances and user movement, further compromise the fidelity of the recorded
signals [12].
In this paper, we analyze the features of wearable EEG signals and explore different ML tech-
niques used to detect mental states. Specifically, this work delves into the classification of Focus, a
crucial cognitive state that reflects sustained attention and mental effort involved in processes such
as imagination, algorithmic reasoning, cognitive engagement or working memory [13–19].
2 Dataset
The public EEG dataset [20]5 consists of recordings from five participants, each of who performed
a passive-control task across up to seven sessions, resulting in a total of 34 sessions (one participant
completed six sessions). EEG signals were recorded at 128 Hz using an Emotiv EPOC headset,
the dataset provides 14 channels (e.g., AF3, F7, F3, FC5, T7, P7, O1, O2, P8, T8, FC6, F4, F8,
AF4). In each session, participants first maintained a focused state for about 10 minutes by actively
monitoring a computer-simulated train, and then entered an unfocused state for roughly 10 minutes
by disengaging from active control. The remaining time was relaxation duration where the subject
closed their eyes and relaxed.
To better understand the dataset and identify a suitable model for real-world applications, we
define three evaluation protocols:
–Session-dependence(5-fold randomsplit):All sessions arepooledtogetherand randomly divided
into training (80%) and testing (20%) sets, without considering session boundaries.
–Session-independence (5-fold by session): The data are split at the session level; entire sessions
are assigned exclusively to either the training or testing set, maintaining an approximate 80/20
ratio.
–Subject-independence: The data are split by subjects such that the training and testing sets
contain disjoint subjects. This protocol evaluates the model’s ability to generalize to unseen
individuals. Since, the dataset contains five subjects, we perform leave-one-subject-out (LOSO)
for testing, remaining four subjects for training in each fold.

3.2 Feature Analysis
We first analyze the correlation between EEG channels to identify potential poor-quality channels
that may adversely affect the back-end model performance. We compute the pairwise Pearson
correlation [21] coefficients across all 14 EEG channels over the entire dataset to quantify the
degree of linear relationship between signals recorded at different scalp locations. A high correlation
between neighboring channels typically reflects consistent neural activity patterns or shared volume
conduction effects, whereas channels exhibiting consistently low correlation with the others may
indicate issues during the data collection process such as poor electrode contact, high impedance,
or motion-induced noise.
The resulting correlation matrix is visualized in Fig. 1. Channels that exhibit low correlation
with the rest are likely unreliable and should be inspected or excluded from further analysis. In
particular, channels such as AF3, FC5, T7, FC4, and F4 demonstrated notably weak correlation
values, suggesting potential issues.
Next, to evaluate the quality of the extracted features, we analyzed their correlation with the
ground-truth (GT) labels provided in the dataset. This analysis allows us to identify which features
are most informative for discriminating between different mental states of ‘Focus’, ‘Unfocus’, and
‘Relaxation’. By computing the Pearson correlation coefficient between each feature and the corre-
sponding GT label, we highlight the features that exhibit the strongest relationships with cognitive
state variations, providing valuable insight into the underlying neural dynamics. The Table 1 and
Fig. 2 summarize the correlation values for 18 features across six selected EEG channels (F7, F3,
P7, O1, O2, and P8), which were chosen based on their higher correlation with GT labels compared
to other channels.

lyze the distribution of features for each cognitive condition. Due to space limitations, we illustrate

4 Model Evaluation
In this section, we explore two types of ML frameworks for mental state classification. The first
category comprises classical ML models, including Decision Trees, Random Forests, Logistic Re-
gression, Support Vector Machines, and extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) [23]. The second
category encompasses deep learning models such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and
recurrent neural networks (RNNs).
Our experiments show that among the classical ML methods, XGBoost consistently achieves the
highest performance across all evaluation protocols. Within the deep learning group, the RNN-based
architecture demonstrates superior accuracy and robustness.
Due to space constraints, we report and discuss only the results of the XGBoost and RNN-based
models in the following.

5 Discussion
OurexperimentsdemonstratethatboththeXGBoostandRNN-basedmodelscaneffectivelyclassify
mental states from EEG data, with the RNN-based model generally outperforming XGBoost in
session-dependent and session-independent evaluations. This suggests that the temporal dynamics
captured by the RNN-based architecture are beneficial for modeling EEG signals.
However, both models exhibit a notable drop in performance under the LOSO protocol, high-
lighting the challenge of subject-to-subject variability in EEG data. This indicates that while the
models can learn patterns within subjects, generalizing these patterns across different individuals
remains challenges. This finding underscores the need for larger and more diverse datasets that in-
clude a greater number of subjects to improve model generalization. Training on only four subjects
and testing on one is insufficient to draw a solid conclusion.
Feature analysis further reveals that specific EEG channels and spectral features contribute
more significantly to the classification performance. This suggests that future research could focus
on optimizing channel selection and feature extraction strategies to enhance the model efficiency
In the LOSO protocol, the lowest performance occurs when Subject 5 is used as the test set. This
likely indicates that Subject 5’s EEG signal distribution differs considerably from other participants,
makingitdifficultforthemodeltoclassify.Thisemphasizestheimportanceofincorporatingsubject-
specific fine-tuning or adaptation strategies to improve real-world robustness in BCI applications.
We further analyze the confusion matrix of the RNN-based model trained on six channels under
the session-independent protocol, as shown in Fig. 6. The model performs best in detecting the
‘Relax’ state, achieving ahigh true positive rate. However, there is substantial confusion between the
‘Focus’ and ‘Unfocus’ states, suggesting that these mental states share overlapping EEG patterns
(consistent with the feature distributions observed in Fig. 3), which complicates discrimination.
